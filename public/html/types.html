Did You Know?

The Enneagram was originally created in 1915 by philosopher George Gurdjieff, the Enneagram was expanded in the late 1960s to include nine personality types. It is now used as a tool for human development and psychological study.

What is Enneagram?

The Enneagram is a personality system, similar to the MBTI, that assists people in better understanding themselves. It provides insights into how people perceive and respond to their surroundings, as well as a framework for personal growth and self-awareness. The system identifies nine main personality types, each with its own style of thinking, feeling, and behaving. Many people believe that learning about the Enneagram helps them have a better grasp of their motivations and relationships.

What are Enneagram Subtypes?

The Enneagram also includes instinctive subtypes, which determine how each personality type appears in the world. These subtypes—Self-Preservation (SP), Social (SO), and Sexual (SX)—reflect fundamental survival instincts that influence our attention, behavior, and priorities. While the primary Enneagram type reveals what motivates us, the subtype demonstrates how that motivation is expressed. Understanding these instincts provides greater understanding into our routines, relationships, and emotional patterns.

1. Self Preservation (SP)

- prioritizes personal safety, comfort, health, and resource management in everyday living. People are more practical, cautious, and concerned about environmental stability.

2. Social (SO)

- emphasizes the need for belonging, status, and connection within organizations or communities. It focuses on how people interact with one another in a social setting, which is frequently expressed as a desire to be respected, useful, or recognized by others.

3. Sexual (SX)

- also known as the one-on-one subtype, focuses on developing deep, intimate connections with people. It emphasizes emotional chemistry, passion, and the desire to merge with someone on a deeper or transforming level.

What are Counter Types (CT)?

Each Enneagram type includes three subtypes, one of which is known as a countertype. This is the subtype that conveys the type's underlying impulses in an unconventional or unusual manner. Countertypes frequently behave differently than what people expect of their type, but sharing the same underlying fear and desire. Recognizing countertypes helps to explain why people with the same Enneagram number might appear completely different from one another.

Enneagram Personality Types

Enneagram Main Types

The Reformer

The Reformer is focused on being good, responsible, and morally upright, with a strong desire to improve both themselves and the world around them. They tend to be organized and principled, but may become overly self-critical when things don’t go as planned.

The Helper

The Helper is motivated by the need to feel loved and appreciated, often showing care and support through helping others. They are warm and generous, though they may neglect their own needs in the process of focusing on others.

The Achiever

The Achiever is driven by the pursuit of success, recognition, and personal achievement, often adapting their image to meet the expectations of others. They are highly motivated and efficient, but may struggle with self-worth tied too closely to performance.

The Individualist

The Individualist seeks to understand their identity through emotional depth and authenticity, often feeling a sense of being different or unique. They are introspective and creative, yet may become preoccupied with longing or what they feel is missing.

The Investigator

The Investigator values knowledge, privacy, and independence, preferring to observe and analyze rather than be emotionally involved. They are curious and intellectually focused, but may withdraw from others to protect their time and energy.

The Loyalist

The Loyalist is centered on security, loyalty, and trust, often scanning for potential risks to stay prepared and safe. They are dependable and committed, though they can become anxious or overly cautious when uncertain.

The Enthusiast

The Enthusiast is oriented toward enjoyment, freedom, and new experiences, using optimism to avoid discomfort or limitation. They are energetic and future-focused, but may struggle with restlessness or avoiding deeper emotions.

The Challenger

The Challenger seeks control, strength, and self-protection, often taking charge in order to avoid vulnerability. They are assertive and protective, but may come across as intense or confrontational at times.

The Peacemaker

The Peacemaker desires harmony, stability, and peace, often going along with others to avoid conflict and maintain a sense of calm. They are supportive and grounded, but can lose sight of their own priorities in an effort to keep the peace.
